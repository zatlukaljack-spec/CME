What is Justice?

Jack Zatlukal

A law is basically just whatever the people in charge decided to write down. Justice is harder to pin down than that and the two don't always line up. This project tries to work through what that gap actually looks like, and what happened when people throughout history pushed back against it.

Introduction

The Relationship Between Law and Justice

Treating law and justice like they're the same thing is a mistake people keep making and the consequences show up all over American history if you actually look.

So what is a law, really? At some basic level it's a rule written by whoever had enough political pull to get it passed, enforced through institutions built by those same people, and shaped by whatever values happened to dominate at the time. Justice is messier than that. It's about fairness, about what we actually owe each other as human beings. Those two things don't automatically line up. And I think assuming they do causes real damage.

Philosophers have been arguing about what justice means forever, but it stops being abstract the second you look at actual American history. Slavery was legal. Keeping women from voting was legal. Segregated schools were legal upheld by the Supreme Court. Criminalizing interracial marriage was legal in sixteen states until 1967. People got arrested for sitting at lunch counters that were, by law, for white customers only. All of it had real legal backing. None of it was right. The people who said so at the time abolitionists, suffragettes, civil rights organizers were right, even when the law treated them as the criminals.

"One has not only a legal but a moral responsibility to obey just laws. Conversely, one has a moral responsibility to disobey unjust laws."

Martin Luther King Jr., Letter from Birmingham Jail, 1963

This project looks at the gap between law and justice from three angles: philosophy, how law actually works as a system, and activism. None of the three is enough on its own. Deontological ethics gives a way to explain why following rules isn't automatically the same as doing right. Looking at how legal systems are actually built shows why they resist change for so long even when everybody can see they're wrong. And the civil rights movement plus a bunch of other movements throughout American history shows what it looks like when people stop waiting for the law to catch up on its own. The argument I'm trying to make across all three is that justice isn't something legal systems just naturally produce. They have to get pushed into it.

Leadership & Ethics

The Philosophy of Justice

There are different ways philosophers approach moral questions, and the one most worth spending time on here is deontology partly because it works a lot like law does. That similarity is interesting, and it's also where the problems start.

Ethics has a handful of main frameworks for thinking through moral decisions. Consequentialism is probably the most recognizable it just looks at outcomes, whether something produced good or bad results. Virtue ethics shifts focus to what kind of person you are, not just what you do. Deontological ethics is different: it's built around rules and duties, the idea that some things are inherently right or wrong no matter what results they end up producing.

What is Deontology?

Kant is the main person associated with deontological ethics. His big principle the Categorical Imperative says you should only act on rules that you could honestly apply to everyone universally without it falling apart. The reason this matters for law specifically is that legal systems run on basically the same logic. They're built around rules, not around outcomes. A law against theft doesn't suddenly stop applying because in a specific situation the theft might have worked out fine for everyone. The rule is the rule. Law thinks the same way. The problem is that neither law nor deontology has a great internal answer to the question of whether the rules themselves were worth anything in the first place. That's the crack in the foundation.

When those principles do make it into law, you get things genuinely worth having due process, equal protection, limits on cruel and unusual punishment. The consistency that deontology requires means, at least in theory, the same rules are supposed to apply to everyone equally. Nobody gets thrown under the bus just because a majority finds it convenient. That's not nothing it's actually a real protection, even if it doesn't always work that way in practice.

Strengths

  • Provides clear, consistent moral rules
  • Protects individual rights from majoritarian override
  • Affirms the inherent dignity of all persons
  • Offers predictability and equal application
  • Resists utilitarian justifications for exploitation

Limitations

  • Rigid rules can produce unjust outcomes in context
  • Treats all rules as equal regardless of their origin
  • Offers no real mechanism for evaluating the justice of the rules themselves
  • Can be exploited to justify oppressive laws as "duty"
  • Fails when the rule-maker is itself unjust

Where Deontology Breaks Down

This is the part where deontology really struggles. It doesn't have any mechanism inside itself for questioning whether the rules it's telling you to follow were good rules to begin with. If the system says follow the law and the law says enforce segregation, nothing within the framework itself pushes back on that. The slave codes of the antebellum South were routinely defended with appeals to duty, order, and the rule of law. People enforcing them were, technically speaking, following the rules. That's not a defense of those people. It's an illustration of what goes wrong when rule-following gets treated like it's automatically the same as doing right.

None of that makes deontology worthless it's genuinely useful, especially the core idea that people have inherent dignity that can't just be overridden. But it means you have to use the framework knowing where it runs out. A rule only has moral weight if the system behind it had some legitimate claim to be making rules. When that claim is missing when the rules were written specifically to exclude the people they'd be applied to the case for following them falls apart pretty quickly.

Legal Writing & Research

How Legal Systems Are Built, and Where They Break

Once you actually look at how law works as a system how precedent gets set, how courts interpret statutes, how slowly any of it changes it's hard to keep treating it like some neutral machine that produces justice automatically. It's built by people, and it shows.

American law is structured in layers. Constitutional provisions are at the top. Below that come federal statutes, regulatory law, and the accumulation of common law that builds up through court rulings over time. State law has its own parallel version of the same thing. When courts decide cases, those decisions become precedent under what's called stare decisis, which basically means that courts in the same jurisdiction are expected to follow the same reasoning when similar questions come up again.

The Doctrine of Precedent

Stare decisis "to stand by things decided" is actually there for real reasons. Consistency in law matters. If courts reversed course constantly, nobody could plan around what the law said, and the whole system would become impossible to navigate. When a court decides something, lower courts in that jurisdiction are supposed to follow it, which at least in theory means people in similar situations get treated the same way.

But here's the problem: once a legal principle gets established even a clearly unjust one it accumulates institutional weight that's very hard to move. Plessy v. Ferguson, from 1896, turned "separate but equal" into constitutional doctrine and gave legal cover to state-mandated segregation for fifty-eight years. That's nearly six decades. And for the whole time, the wrongness of what Plessy allowed wasn't some obscure contested question it was obvious to huge numbers of people, including obviously most of the people actually forced to live under it. But it was precedent. And precedent has a kind of gravity that resists correction even when the moral case for correcting it is completely obvious. A court ruling doesn't get moral authority just because it exists, but the legal system more or less acts like it does. That gap is where a lot of harm accumulates over a long time.

Legal Legitimacy vs. Moral Authority

These are genuinely two different things, and mixing them up is where a lot of problems start. Legal legitimacy just means something is authorized under the existing rules. Moral authority asks whether the framework behind those rules was actually justified in the first place which is a much harder question. Jim Crow laws were legally legitimate. The Fugitive Slave Act was legally legitimate. Every anti-miscegenation law on the books had been upheld by courts. None of that gave them moral authority. Every one of them eventually got struck down, not because the legal system worked it out on its own, but because people refused to accept that "it's the law" settled the question of whether it was right.

How Unjust Laws Get Challenged

The formal legal channels do exist constitutional litigation, legislation, appellate review. But they're slow, and I don't think that's entirely by accident. You need plaintiffs who are willing to absorb the cost and risk of bringing a case. You need courts willing to actually hear it. And for real, structural change, you need a majority of judges willing to break from established precedent. That's a lot to align at once, and it tends to be hardest to pull off precisely when injustice is most dug in because the same power structures that wrote the unjust law also shaped the institutions that would theoretically undo it. By 1954, when the Court overturned Plessy in Brown v. Board of Education, the moral argument against segregation had been obvious for generations. The law moved because the NAACP spent decades building a litigation strategy specifically designed to create the conditions for that ruling. Not because courts figured it out on their own schedule.

Civil disobedience keeps showing up throughout American history because it's not a last resort it's a pretty logical response to how the system actually works. When the formal channels are too slow, too expensive, or controlled by the same people who benefit from things staying as they are, outside pressure becomes the only realistic option. The civil rights movement figured this out and built a whole strategy around it.

Black Music & Activism

When People Fight Back

The civil rights movement wasn't just a legal campaign. It was a sustained argument made in streets, in churches, in courtrooms, in music that what the law said was wrong. And that it had to change, whether the legal system was ready for that or not.

The civil rights movement of the 1950s and 60s wasn't a polite request for the system to be kinder. It was a direct confrontation with a legal order specifically built to exclude Black Americans from full participation in American life. The people leading it weren't naive about that they understood exactly what they were up against. They weren't asking the system to be nicer. They were forcing it to answer for what it actually was versus what it claimed to be.

Martin Luther King Jr. and the Moral Case for Disobedience

King's Letter from Birmingham Jail is something most people have heard of, but I think it's more philosophically sophisticated than people usually give it credit for. He wasn't just saying that bad laws should be broken. He was pulling on Augustine, Aquinas, and natural law theory to build a specific argument: that an unjust law doesn't actually carry moral authority, because the obligation to obey law comes from whether that law lines up with a higher moral standard. When it doesn't, the obligation is gone. He also insisted and this part matters that civil disobedience required accepting the legal consequences. That wasn't an afterthought. It was a deliberate statement: the challenge was to specific unjust laws, not to the idea of law itself.

It's worth actually picturing what that looked like: a man writing what might be the most important moral document of the twentieth century from inside a jail cell, put there for marching. The charges were real. He was actually imprisoned. And his response wasn't to ask for sympathy or appeal to people's basic decency. It was to lay out, in careful detail, exactly why the law that locked him up had no legitimate claim on his obedience. That's a different kind of argument.

"An unjust law is a code that a numerical or power majority group compels a minority group to obey but does not make binding on itself."

Martin Luther King Jr., Letter from Birmingham Jail, 1963

Malcolm X and the Limits of Moral Persuasion

Malcolm X gets footnoted a lot in discussions like this, but his actual critique is worth sitting with. He wasn't saying justice was impossible or that King was wrong about morality. He was making a more structural argument: that appealing to the conscience of a system that was functioning exactly as designed puts all the burden on the people being harmed, while leaving the power structures doing the harm completely in place. Waiting for moral persuasion to move institutions that had no real reason to move was, in his view, a strategy that ended up benefiting the people in power more than anyone else. And his specific point about the law is important it wasn't failing Black Americans through some oversight. It was built to produce the outcomes it was producing. That's a very different claim, and not an easy one to dismiss.

It's worth holding both of those in mind at the same time, even though they're in tension. King explains why civil disobedience is morally coherent why it's not just lawlessness with better PR. Malcolm X explains why moral coherence alone doesn't move things, and why you can't just keep deferring justice while waiting for powerful people to decide they're ready. The movement needed both of those pressures simultaneously. The legal changes that actually came out of it came from both not just from the version that white America found more comfortable to talk about.

Connections to Other Struggles

The same pattern shows up in other places too, once you start looking for it. Women fighting for suffrage were working against a legal structure that cut them out of political life entirely and the official process for changing that structure required buy-in from the exact people who benefited from keeping them out. Early twentieth century labor organizers kept running into laws that treated collective bargaining as criminal conspiracy. LGBTQ+ Americans were criminalized for who they were well into the second half of the century, and the official legal system offered them almost nothing for decades. In all of these cases, legal legitimacy and moral authority were pointing in opposite directions. And in all of them, actual progress required people willing to push against the law rather than just work through it.

Case Studies

Three Moments When Law and Justice Collided

These three cases are different from each other in obvious ways, but they're all making the same basic point from different angles: legal legitimacy and moral authority aren't the same thing, and history has usually come down on the side of the people who understood that distinction.

01
Case Study
Brown v. Board of Education (1954)
Legal Writing Black Music & Activism Leadership & Ethics

Plessy v. Ferguson didn't invent segregation it was already happening in practice across most of the country. What Plessy did was hand it constitutional backing. It dressed segregation in the fiction that separate facilities could be equal and made it much harder to challenge through the same legal system that had just signed off on it. State legislatures then built entire apartheid structures on top of that ruling, and those structures had the full weight of law, courts, and enforcement behind them for the next fifty-eight years.

Brown v. Board required the Supreme Court to do something courts genuinely resist doing: admit that a prior Court got it wrong. Warren's unanimous opinion said that separate educational facilities are inherently unequal. And inside that legal finding is a moral one that segregation stigmatizes and demeans kids regardless of whether the buildings look similar. The harm wasn't some accidental side effect. It was baked into the whole logic of separate-but-equal from the beginning.

From a Kantian standpoint, the whole Plessy regime fails pretty straightforwardly the principle that people shouldn't be treated as mere means was being violated systematically, by statute, for generations. But that philosophical failure didn't fix anything on its own. The Court's correction in Brown happened because the NAACP built a litigation strategy over decades, plaintiff by plaintiff, case by case, specifically designed to create the conditions for that ruling. The legal system didn't correct itself. It was corrected from outside.

02
Case Study
The Birmingham Campaign (1963)
Black Music & Activism Legal Writing Leadership & Ethics

Birmingham in 1963 wasn't a random choice. It was one of the most aggressively segregated cities in the country, and Bull Connor the Public Safety Commissioner had a very well established reputation for using whatever force the law allowed against anyone who challenged that order. The SCLC knew what they were getting into. The campaign was specifically designed to produce a confrontation, because a confrontation was what would make the situation impossible for a national audience to keep ignoring.

The charges against protesters unlawful assembly, trespassing, parading without a permit were technically valid under existing ordinances. The law was working exactly the way it was written to work. The problem was what it had been written to do: protect a social order built on racial subjugation, using technically legal tools to suppress political opposition. King, writing from the jail cell those charges put him in, used Augustine and Aquinas to explain why a law imposed by a majority on a minority that had zero real voice in making it fails at the most fundamental level. A just law builds up human dignity, he argued. Segregation laws were doing the opposite on purpose.

The Birmingham Campaign produced federal intervention and fed directly into the Civil Rights Act of 1964. But it wasn't just the moral argument that got things moving. It was the moral argument made physically visible made costly, made impossible to look away from on television that created the political conditions for Congress to act. Bull Connor turning fire hoses on children moved more people than any amount of legal briefs ever could have.

03
Case Study
Loving v. Virginia (1967)
Legal Writing Leadership & Ethics Black Music & Activism

Richard and Mildred Loving got married in Washington D.C. in 1958 and moved back to Virginia, where their marriage was literally a criminal act under the Racial Integrity Act of 1924. They were arrested in their bedroom, convicted, and sentenced to a year in prison with the sentence suspended only if they left the state for twenty-five years. The judge, Leon Bazile, explained his reasoning by saying God had put the races on different continents for a reason, and that mixing them went against his plan. That's not a paraphrase. That was the stated moral foundation of the law, from one of the people responsible for enforcing it.

The Racial Integrity Act had been passed by a legislature, signed by a governor, upheld by courts multiple times, and was still active law in sixteen states when the Lovings were arrested. Fully legitimate as legislation goes. Also completely indefensible on any serious ethical ground I can think of. Kant's principle that people should be treated as ends in themselves, never as mere means gets violated pretty directly when you criminalize a marriage based on how the state classifies the race of the people in it. You're reducing people to categories, not treating them as individuals, in one of the most personal decisions a person can make.

The Supreme Court's unanimous 1967 ruling struck down every remaining anti-miscegenation law in the country. Warren wrote that marriage is one of the basic civil rights of man and that restricting it by race violates both the Equal Protection and Due Process Clauses. But here's what I keep coming back to: the Fourteenth Amendment was ratified in 1868. The legal tools to invalidate laws like the Racial Integrity Act had existed for almost a hundred years before anybody used them for that purpose. The law didn't finally move because the legal argument became available. It moved because the people being harmed by it forced the question into a court that couldn't dodge it anymore.

Conclusion & Reflection

What Justice Actually Requires

Looking at all of this, the pattern is pretty consistent. Justice isn't something legal systems just generate on their own. It gets pushed out of them by people who wouldn't let "it's the law" be the end of the conversation.

Brown, Birmingham, and Loving are three different situations, but they're making the same argument across different decades and different contexts. Legal systems reflect the values and priorities of whoever built them. When those builders excluded entire groups from any meaningful role in the process, the laws that came out of it reflected that exclusion pretty faithfully. And the correction in each case didn't come from the legal system recognizing its own mistakes. It came from sustained outside pressure from litigation, from protest, from people who refused to accept that legal validity answered the moral question.

Why This Needs More Than One Lens

Using philosophy, law, and history together produces something that none of them reaches on its own. Deontological ethics gives a basis for explaining why following rules isn't automatically the same as being moral and where rule-based thinking hits a wall when the rules were written by people who didn't have the moral authority to write them. Looking at how law actually functions as a system reveals that legal institutions are structurally designed to stay the same. Stare decisis serves real purposes, but it also works as a preservation mechanism for injustice long after anyone can honestly defend it. And history and activism fill in the part that philosophy and legal theory can't really reach: what actually makes things change, which turns out to be external pressure, not the internal reasoning of courts.

What Philosophy Contributes

Deontological ethics gives a framework for separating just rules from unjust ones and insists that human dignity is not a negotiable variable. Its weakness is that it offers no internal check on the legitimacy of whoever wrote the rules. Moral authority has to come before legal authority not after it.

What Legal Study Contributes

Understanding how legal systems are built reveals that their resistance to change is structural, not accidental. Precedent protects stability, and stability is not the same thing as justice. Knowing how law works matters because it shows both how to challenge it and why formal channels alone have historically been insufficient to produce real change.

What Activism Contributes

The civil rights movement shows that moral argument without political pressure doesn't move legal systems. King and Malcolm X disagreed sharply on strategy, but neither believed waiting on institutional goodwill was a viable approach. The historical record consistently backs that up.

The Integrated Argument

Together, these disciplines make a coherent case: law and justice are related but not identical, legal systems can and do produce serious injustice, and when they do, the moral weight falls on the side of challenge rather than compliance. The cases examined here show how that plays out when it actually matters.

The Stance This Project Takes

The argument I've been building throughout this project is that there's no genuine moral obligation to obey an unjust law. I want to be clear that this isn't some fringe view look at who's held it. King held it. Thoreau held it. The suffragettes held it. The NAACP lawyers who constructed the litigation strategy that eventually produced Brown held it. Every court that has ever struck down a law on constitutional grounds was operating, in practice, on the same basic logic: that a law being passed doesn't by itself make it morally legitimate. What makes a law worth following is whether it actually conforms to something recognizable as fairness equal treatment, basic human dignity. The existence of the law isn't enough on its own.

The alternative that law demands obedience just because it's the law isn't just philosophically weak. It's the exact reasoning that sustained segregation for nearly sixty years after Plessy. It kept interracial marriage illegal in sixteen states until 1967. If you actually apply it consistently, it demands compliance with every injustice that ever made it into statute. The people who rejected that reasoning are the specific reason any of those injustices ever got fixed.

Justice isn't something law comes built with. It's a standard that has to be applied to the law from outside by people willing to pay real costs to do it. The Fourteenth Amendment was on the books from 1868. The Loving decision came down in 1967. That's ninety-nine years during which the legal tools to strike down anti-miscegenation laws already existed and nobody used them for that. What changed wasn't the legal framework. It was the pressure people applied to it. I think that's worth holding onto when evaluating what legal systems can actually do on their own and what they need before they'll move.
